01 The Product

02 The Problem

Compliance knowledge lives in PDFs. Compliance skill doesn't.

Everything about the EU AI Act and GDPR is written down somewhere — the regulations, EDPB guidance, a thousand law-firm explainers. But the practical skills — classifying a real system into a risk tier, running a DPIA that survives scrutiny — are learned on the job, because reading about a decision procedure is nothing like executing one.

I couldn't find a tool that trained those skills without expensive corporate licensing. So I built one — for myself first, then for anyone.

03 What It Does

Commit first. Understand second. Remember forever.

  • Risk classification trainer. Realistic system descriptions — a CV-screening model, emotion recognition in a call centre, a disclosed chatbot — each demanding a tier: prohibited, high, limited, or minimal. You answer before you see the Article 5 / Annex III reasoning, including why the plausible wrong answers are wrong.
  • DPIA walkthrough. The full Article 35 structure: describing processing, necessity and proportionality, risks to rights and freedoms, mitigations — with weak and strong sample answers side by side.
  • Governance frameworks reference. Three lines of defence, provider vs. deployer obligations, and how NIST AI RMF's govern–map–measure–manage cycle maps onto Act requirements.
04 Built With Claude

Constraints as design — enforced by a very literal collaborator.

Three deliberate constraints shaped the build: no build tools (open index.html and it runs, forever), no API costs (all logic client-side, shareable with a whole cohort for free), and no backend (progress in localStorage; no account, no tracking, no personal data leaving the device — the app practises the data minimisation it teaches).

Working with Claude, the architecture stayed honest: scenario content lives in plain JSON, fully separated from the engine, so adding a scenario is a content task a lawyer could do, not a programming task. The hardest work was writing scenarios whose answers I could defend against a lawyer's objection — and drafting those reasoning chains with Claude, then stress-testing them against the actual regulation text, was the deepest EU AI Act study I've ever done.

"A tool that teaches GDPR should be the one thing in your browser that provably isn't collecting anything."

07 What I Learned

Writing the scenarios was harder than writing the code.

Key Takeaways
  • Interactive beats declarative for regulatory training. Committing to an answer before seeing the reasoning is where learning happens.
  • Constraints compound. No build, no backend, no cost made the app durable, forkable and private — properties a compliance tool should have anyway.
  • Encoding law audits both the law and the encoder. Every fuzzy branch in the decision tree marked a fuzzy patch in my understanding — or in the Act.

Planned next: a GPAI-obligations module and a scenario pack on deployer transparency duties. The app is static — it deploys anywhere that serves files.
